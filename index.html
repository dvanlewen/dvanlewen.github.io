About Me

I am a PhD candidate in Mechanical Engineering at Boston University in the Material Robotics Lab.
I am passionate about robotics and its applications in solving human-facing problems.
My PhD research focuses on developing soft robotic platforms for neurosurgery and bronchoscopy that can safely interact with and navigate their respective environments.

Research

My research focuses on the development of soft robots that will pave the way towards enodscopic procedures that are safer, more effective, and more accessible. I investigate this through the design, fabrication, and control of soft robots that can effectively interact with and navigate the surrounding anatomical environment. These goals are realized in my two projects: (1) a Soft Robotic Bronchoscope with semi-autonomous navigation and safe biopsy/treatment of lung tumors and (2) an Origami-Inspired Soft Robotic Retractor for accessing deep regions of the brain and improving safety via force monitoring in neurosurgery.

Soft Robotic Bronchoscopy

The soft robotic bronchoscope was developed to address the world's deadliest form of cancer: lung cancer. The robot I created incorporates 3 independent fluidic actuators within a 3mm diameter soft continuum robot. These actuators enable: steering, stabilization, and needle deployment. In combining these, I created a robot that has the necessary size, material compliance, and dexterity to navigate (steering) into the peripheral regions of the lungs, where most tumors form, while still producing the necessary forces (stabilization) to obtain a biopsy sample (needle deployment).

This work was published in the journal Advanced Intelligent Systems and was featured in Advanced Science News and local BU media. I also presented it at the Hamlyn Symposium on Medical Robotics 2023 (London) and MRS Spring 2023 (San Francisco).

This video provides a demonstration of each fluidic actuator, a close up look at the needle deployment actuator, and the envisioned workflow demonstrated in-vitro.

I have improved the robot in both hardware and software to enhance navigation, reduce surgeon cognitive load and make procedures overall more efficient. I incorporated a 1mm microcamera for visualization and electromagnetic tracking for localization into a miniaturized 2.5mm soft continuum robot. The robot can steer its tip in 1 direction and be inserted/rotated/translated from outside the patient via an external driving system.

On the software side, I developed a closed loop, semi-autonomous navigation algorithm for controlling the robot with the surgeon in a supervisory role. I accomplished this using ROS2 to create a custom communication protocol between sensors and actuators. Relying on YOLO image processing, localization, and preoperative path planning, the robot commands each actuator to navigate to within 1mm of the target location. A teleoperation controller was also developed for the surgeon to directly interface with the robot at any time during a procedure.

Origami-Inspired Neurosurgical Retractor

The soft robotic retractor is a Miura origami patterned folding robot that is collapsible by applying vacuum pressures within the film encasing it. A positive pressure actuator embedded within the robot stiffens it to sustain a force output. Its purpose is to open a region in the brain by displacing (i.e. retracting) tissue in a safer manner than traditional rigid, stainless steel retractors. This robot allows surgical tools to access deeper seated lesions within neurosurgery and enables a minimally invasive approach as it can be collapsed in size, inserted into a bore hole, expanded, and then stiffened to retract tissue.

This work was published in Frontiers in Robotics and AI.

To increase safety and provide greater information to the neurosurgeon, I created origami sensing modules (OSM). They are soft, foldable sensors in the shape of a single Miura unit cell that rely on the principle of varying capacitance. The capacitance signal responds to changes in the robot shape and output forces. I developed a prediction model that can determine the level of force applied on surrounding tissue regardless of shape. The OSM is able to measure forces with a 0.25 N or 0.5 kPa resolution which is essential for preventing unnecessary damage to tissue.

I presented this work at IEEE ICRA 2024 (Yokohama), Hamlyn Symposium on Medical Robotics 2024, and MRS Fall 2024 (Boston).
